      Lens Guide

      A quick, plain-language overview of the lens types you will see across the collection and inside the constructor.

      Clear

      Standard optical lenses with no tint. A neutral everyday choice — they simply correct (or protect) without changing how colors look.

      Blue Light

      Lenses with a coating that filters part of the high-energy blue-violet range emitted by screens. Many people who spend long hours in front of monitors choose them for visual comfort; the faint residual tint is barely noticeable.

      Sunglasses

      Tinted lenses that reduce overall brightness. Tint darkness affects glare and comfort, while UV protection comes from the lens material and coatings — a lens can be dark without blocking UV, so the two should not be confused.

      Polarized

      Polarized lenses contain a filter that blocks horizontally reflected light — the harsh glare that bounces off water, snow, car hoods and wet roads. The result is noticeably crisper contrast outdoors. One trade-off: some LCD screens can look dim or patchy through a polarizing filter.

      Photochromic

      Light-adaptive lenses that darken in sunlight and return to near-clear indoors. They react to UV exposure, so they darken less behind a car windshield, which already blocks most UV.

      Frame Size Guide

      Three numbers describe almost every pair of glasses. Here is how to read them — and how to measure a frame you already love.

      The Three Key Measurements

      • Lens width — the horizontal width of one lens, typically 40–62 mm.
      • Bridge width — the gap between the lenses where the frame rests on your nose, typically 14–24 mm.
      • Temple length — the arm from hinge to tip, most commonly 135–150 mm.

      Where to Find the Numbers

      Most frames print their measurements on the inside of a temple arm in the form 52 □ 19 145 — lens width, bridge width and temple length in millimeters. If you already own glasses that fit well, those three numbers are the best starting point for your next pair.

      Measuring Without a Reference Pair

      Use a millimeter ruler in front of a mirror: measure the distance between your temples (the flat areas beside your eyes) and compare it with a frame's total width — frame width close to your face width usually means a comfortable fit. As a loose rule of thumb, total frame widths under about 130 mm suit narrower faces, 130–138 mm suit most, and above 138 mm suit wider faces.
